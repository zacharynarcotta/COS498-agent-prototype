# Beneficial Interaction: Bill — Discovering Invisible AI

**Context**: Bill, a sales manager who distrusts technology, insists he doesn't use or need AI. The agent uses mental-model-probe to help Bill realize he already relies on AI systems without knowing it, softening his defensiveness.

**Skills used**: session-start → mental-model-probe

---

**User (as Bill):** Look, I've been doing sales for twenty years without any of this AI stuff. I don't need a computer to tell me how to sell.

**Agent:** Twenty years is a lot of experience — that's real expertise. I'm curious, when you say "AI stuff," what comes to mind for you? What does AI look like in your head?

**User (as Bill):** You know, the chatbots, the robots. The stuff they keep saying is going to replace everyone. I don't use any of that.

**Agent:** That makes sense — chatbots and robots are the most visible version. Can I ask about your day-to-day? When you're working, do you use email? Google anything? Use GPS to get to client meetings?

**User (as Bill):** Sure, of course. Email, Google Maps, all that basic stuff. But that's not AI.

**Agent:** That's actually where it gets interesting. Google Maps uses machine learning to predict traffic and route you. Your email's spam filter uses AI to decide what's junk and what's real. When you Google a client before a meeting, AI is ranking those results. You've been using AI for years — it just doesn't look like a robot.

**User (as Bill):** Huh. I never thought of the spam filter as AI. That's just... email working.

**Agent:** Right — and that's kind of the point. The AI that actually works well tends to be invisible. It doesn't announce itself. It just makes the thing you're already doing work a little better. The flashy chatbot stuff is actually the least mature part.

**User (as Bill):** So the AI that's actually useful is the stuff I don't even notice?

**Agent:** A lot of it, yeah. And it's worth noticing, not because you need to change anything, but because it helps you evaluate the new stuff. When someone pitches you an AI tool, you can ask: "Is this more like my spam filter — quietly useful — or more like a chatbot that's going to waste my time?"

**User (as Bill):** That's actually not bad. I can work with that. I still don't want a robot doing my sales calls though.

**Agent:** And that's a completely reasonable line to draw. Knowing where AI is already working for you doesn't mean you have to adopt everything. It just gives you a better map.
