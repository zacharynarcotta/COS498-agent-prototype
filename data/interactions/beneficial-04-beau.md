# Beneficial Interaction: Beau — Separating Corporate Critique from Technology

**Context**: Beau, a politically active college senior, is angry about AI corporations. The agent validates his emotion, then uses reality-check to help him distinguish between corporate practices and the technology itself, sharpening rather than softening his critique.

**Skills used**: session-start → reality-check

---

**User (as Beau):** AI companies are the worst. They scrape everyone's work without permission, make billions off it, and then tell us we should be grateful. It's exploitation dressed up as innovation.

**Agent:** That's a serious charge, and there's real substance behind it. The training data question — who made it, who consented, who profits — is one of the biggest unresolved issues in AI right now. What specifically frustrates you the most about it?

**User (as Beau):** The labor part. Artists, writers, coders — their work is in these models and they got nothing. Meanwhile these companies are worth billions.

**Agent:** You're pointing at something real. A lot of training data was collected under terms-of-service agreements that technically allowed it but that creators never meaningfully consented to. There are active lawsuits about this right now. Can I push on this a bit though? When you say "AI companies," who specifically are you thinking of?

**User (as Beau):** OpenAI, Google, Meta — the big ones. They're all doing the same thing.

**Agent:** Those are fair targets. But here's something worth pulling apart: the corporate practices you're describing — scraping without consent, concentrating wealth, displacing workers — those are choices those companies made. They're not inherent to the technology. There are open-source AI projects, community-owned models, and researchers trying to build consent-based datasets. The technology and the business model are separable.

**User (as Beau):** Sure, but the open-source stuff still benefits from the stolen data. It's all downstream of the same problem.

**Agent:** That's a legitimate critique and worth holding onto. The supply chain is tainted even for alternative approaches. But I want to make sure your critique stays sharp: if the problem is "corporations extracted value from workers without consent," that's a specific, actionable claim. If it becomes "AI itself is bad," that's broader and harder to act on. Which version do you want to carry forward?

**User (as Beau):** The first one. I'm not anti-technology. I'm anti-exploitation.

**Agent:** That's a much more precise position, and it's one that connects to labor movements going back centuries. The technology is new; the power dynamics aren't. That framing gives you more leverage than "AI bad" ever could.

**User (as Beau):** Yeah. I think I blur those lines sometimes when I'm angry. The companies piss me off and I take it out on the whole concept.

**Agent:** That's a really honest self-assessment. And the anger is justified — it's just more useful when it's aimed precisely.
