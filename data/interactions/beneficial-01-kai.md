# Beneficial Interaction: Kai — Decomposing a Capability Claim

**Context**: Kai, a UX designer, opens by claiming AI gives better design feedback than his human colleagues. The agent uses reality-check to help Kai decompose the claim and arrive at a more nuanced understanding.

**Skills used**: session-start → reality-check

---

**User (as Kai):** Honestly, I've been getting better UX feedback from ChatGPT than from my actual team. It's faster, more detailed, and doesn't have ego problems.

**Agent:** That's an interesting observation — and I can see why speed and detail would matter a lot in your workflow. Can I ask what kind of feedback you're comparing? Like, is this about visual layout, user flow logic, accessibility, or something else?

**User (as Kai):** Mostly layout and copy. I'll paste a wireframe description and it gives me solid suggestions in seconds.

**Agent:** That makes sense — for pattern-based suggestions on layout and copy, AI can pull from a huge range of examples really quickly. What about the feedback your team gives you — what does that usually look like?

**User (as Kai):** Slower, for sure. And sometimes it's just "looks good" with no detail. But occasionally someone catches something I completely missed.

**Agent:** So the AI is more consistent and faster, but your team sometimes catches things the AI doesn't. What kinds of things do they catch that the AI misses?

**User (as Kai):** Hmm... usually stuff about our specific users. Like, "this won't work for the older demographic we're targeting" or "legal will flag this copy." The AI doesn't know our users.

**Agent:** That's a really important distinction. The AI is drawing from general UX patterns — it's great at "does this follow common design principles." But your team knows your specific users, your company's constraints, your legal environment. Those are things that aren't in any training dataset.

**User (as Kai):** Yeah, I guess "better feedback" isn't one thing. The AI is better at some parts and my team is better at others.

**Agent:** Exactly. And neither one replaces the other — they're answering different questions. The AI asks "does this match known patterns?" Your team asks "does this work for our people?" Both matter.

**User (as Kai):** That actually changes how I think about using it. I've been treating AI feedback as a replacement when it's more like a first pass.

**Agent:** That's a really useful reframe. A fast first pass that catches pattern-level issues, freeing your team to focus on the contextual stuff only they can see.
